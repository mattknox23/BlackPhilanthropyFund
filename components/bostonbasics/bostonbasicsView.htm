The Boston Basics offers strategies to all parents of young children. It is a research-based set of parenting principles and effective parenting skills developed by Professor Ron Ferguson and the research of the Achievement Gap Initiative at Harvard. The five fundamental principles are:

  1. Maximize Love, Manage Stress. Build a positive, supportive bond with your child. Maximize loving responsiveness and manage stress for both child and parents beginning at birth.
  2. Talk, Sing and Point. Use real talk, not baby talk, from the very beginning. Use lots of gestures—pointing and being physically expressive.
  3. Count, Group, and Compare. Use activities that develop mathematical reasoning, for example, order, sequence, magnitude and time.
  4. Explore Through Movement and Play. Think of physical spaces as opportunities to play and learn. Encourage maneuvering (crawling and walking) in cluttered spaces, and manipulating three-dimensional toys and objects.
  5. Read and Discuss Stories. Read even before the child can understand or respond. Discuss stories daily. Take active walks through the neighborhood and/or the store. Point and discuss while driving. Cultivate a love of learning through conversations as you read, live, walk and ride.

The Boston Basics is a framework for collective action on effective early childhood education in our community, where effective parenting strategies move from knowledge, to understanding, to behavior, to culture—and thus become part of the fabric of the community. The campaign objective: dramatically increase kindergarten readiness, setting a foundation for college and career readiness for generations of our youth.
